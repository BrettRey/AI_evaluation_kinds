2026-05-15 — Outline reframed from a typology of *terms* to a typology of *inference licences*, after a repo-level review and an eight-reviewer board (`notes/review-board-2026-05-15.md`). Governing question is now "what inferences does each term license, and what makes them projectible?" `OUTLINE.md` restructured to nine sections: construct validity (§2) and Boyd/Khalidi projectibility (§3) are now separate sections ahead of the typology table (§4). Type 1 relabelled "output-centred failure profile"; *robustness* narrowed to *jailbreak resistance*; the capability case rebuilt with *reasoning* as the main case (controlled variation), *emergence* as the metric-dependence case, *agency* as the system-boundary case. Working title now "From Scores to Kinds: Projectibility and Validity in AI Evaluation." Reason: protects the contribution from being read as a familiar construct-validity complaint, and makes projectibility the payoff rather than a footnote.

2026-05-15 — Outline issue pass completed. 2026-05-16 — Stop local polishing of the current zero draft. Reason: after a full read, Brett and Codex agreed the draft is clear but underwhelming; its current centre is a tidy typology rather than a sufficiently sharp philosophical claim. Future work should recast the paper around the missing projectibility norm in AI evaluation: reliability can show stability, construct validity can show what a score means in the measured setting, but neither tells us when claims may travel across models, prompts, attacks, benchmarks, or governance uses. The typology should become a diagnostic tool serving that thesis, not the thesis itself.

2026-05-28 — Reading note: Weinberger (2026), "Homeostasis and causal control" (`weinberger2026HomeostasisCausalControl`). Use in the structural rewrite to separate stable benchmark performance from controlled robustness. Reliability can show that a score or behaviour is stable; Weinberger helps ask whether the evaluation ecosystem has a control architecture that corrects perturbation across models, prompts, attacks, and deployment contexts.
